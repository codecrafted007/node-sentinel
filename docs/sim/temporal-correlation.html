The averaging trap

A short, intense burst by one pod can ruin a node — but if your agent only reads a counter every few seconds, the burst is averaged away. The same data, bucketed at sub-interval resolution, keeps the spike visible.

Drag bucket size up toward the scrape interval and watch the spike melt into the average — that's exactly what a coarse counter does. Shrink it and the burst reappears. This is why the kernel keeps a small ring of sub-interval buckets instead of one number per interval.

Co-occurrence, not magnitude

Two pods on the same node, 50 buckets of 100 ms. The offender bursts; the victim's run-queue stalls a beat later. We score the shape agreement with a lagged Pearson correlation — so a legitimately busy pod that hurts no one scores low, and a moderate-but-disruptive pod scores high.

Turn coupling to 0 and crank the offender height: huge magnitude, but no correlation → low confidence. That's the whole point — busy ≠ guilty. Now turn independent victim spike up: the victim is suffering, but not from this pod, and the gate plus correlation refuse to blame it. Drop active buckets low and a single coincident blip can sneak through — which is why the real gate stays strict.
